Safety guide for downwind foiling

Downwind foiling (with a parawing or SUP foil) is incredibly rewarding, but it can also be dangerous. Especially for beginners, wind strength, changing conditions, and proper planning play a crucial role. With good preparation, the right equipment, and respect for the conditions, downwind runs can remain controlled, safe, and enjoyable.

This guide focuses on practical safety rules for downwind foiling, including preparation, self-rescue, and emergency situations.

Preparation (MUST-DO CHECKLIST)

  • If possible, do not go downwind foiling alone â†’ find & add you local spots and crew in our foiling map
  • Use a board leash and inspect it before every run
  • Take a phone with you (waterproofed) and share your live location with someone
  • Tell someone your plan (route, expected exit point, and timing)
  • Wear the correct neoprene suit for the water temperature
  • In cold conditions: pack closed neoprene gloves in case you need to paddle back by hand
  • In hot conditions: use sun protection
  • Wear a personal flotation device (PFD) with a whistle and light
  • Bring food (fast energy) and drinking water
  • Carry a tourniquet for deep foil cuts
  • Extra safety: bring a personal locator beacon (PLB) with a strobe light
  • Extra safety: wear a helmet
  • Extra safety: wear something brightly colored
  • Check the weather forecast (temperature, swell, wind, lightning) and daylight hours
  • Prefer short, safe runs over one long and committing run, especially in winter or strong winds
  • Bonus tip for the ocean: bring a line cutter knife

Have a Plan B (Self-Rescue)

Your paddle can break. Your parawing, foil, or even your board can be lost. Always assume that something may fail.

  • Stay within realistic paddling distance of shore (for most this is less than 2 km)
  • Keep enough energy reserve for self-rescue
  • If everything fails, you must be able to swim or paddle back to shore
  • After a fall, take time to recover: stay calm, breathe, reset
  • Do not rush back onto foil when tired or stressed
  • When in doubt, abort early

In Case of Emergency

If all preparation fails:

  • Stay with your board whenever possible
  • Call 112 (works in most countries worldwide) or 911 (US, Canada, Mexico) and have your coordinates ready.
  • Use your whistle, light, or strobe
  • Wave both arms above your head to signal for help
  • Use your PLB if everything else fails.

This downwind foiling safety guide is open source and can be found on GitHub. We welcome your contributions via pull requests or by sending ideas and feedback via email.
